# Reflection on AI in the Alx Voting System Project

This project, the **Alx Voting System**, was my first time fully integrating AI into a complete full-stack build. Looking back, I can see clearly how AI accelerated the development process, where it felt limiting, and how my understanding of prompting, reviewing, and iterating improved as we went along.

From the start, AI provided incredible value in **breaking down complex tasks** into actionable steps. For example, designing the system architecture with Next.js, Supabase, and Vercel could have taken hours of research. Instead, AI gave me a well-thought-out structure for authentication, database schema, and route protection in minutes. It even suggested best practices like using **Row Level Security (RLS)** in Supabase and middleware in Next.js for authorization. This kind of scaffolding allowed me to stay focused on building instead of constantly switching to documentation.

One major area where AI shined was **boilerplate code generation**. Writing the Supabase schema for `profiles`, `polls`, `votes`, and `poll_options` tables was fast and accurate. It even created SQL policies for RLS, which I later tested and confirmed worked as expected. The **login/signup pages** were also generated with TailwindCSS styling included, saving me hours of UI tweaking. Whenever I needed a redirect, an admin check, or error handling, AI gave me a working example that I could adapt quickly.

However, I also ran into **limitations**. At first, I expected AI to handle everything perfectly, but I learned that database operations involving RLS and authentication timing require deeper understanding. For example, the `profiles.insert()` issue after signup taught me that AI can give correct code syntactically, but I still need to reason about **auth contexts** and **security implications**. The solution—using RPC functions with `SECURITY DEFINER` or Postgres triggers—came after I explicitly asked AI for alternatives. This showed me that **specific prompts lead to better results**. Saying “fix my insert issue” got me partial answers, but saying “explain why inserting profiles after signup fails with RLS and give three solutions” brought clarity and better options.

Another learning point was **iterative refinement**. The first version of the login/signup page was functional but lacked styling and branding. After asking AI to “make it professional and responsive with Tailwind,” the second version looked production-ready. The same happened with middleware: the initial code worked but didn’t handle admin routes until I explicitly asked for role-based access. This taught me to treat AI as a **pair programmer**—start broad, then refine details through multiple iterations rather than expecting perfection in one go.

Overall, using AI turned what could have been a multi-week build into a structured, learning-focused process. I learned to write **clear, contextual prompts**, to **review AI output critically**, and to **iterate quickly** when something didn’t feel right. The combination of speed, flexibility, and the need for human oversight made this experience both efficient and educational. Next time, I’ll approach AI not just as a coding assistant but as a collaborative tool for design, debugging, and documentation.
